Data Controllers vs Data Processors in Kenya: Understanding the Difference

An analytical look at regulatory classifications, liability boundaries, and legal accountability distributions under the Kenyan Data Protection Act, 2019.

Speak to a Lawyer

The distinction between a data controller and a data processor is one of the most important concepts under Kenya’s Data Protection Act, 2019.

Many businesses assume these terms apply only to large technology companies. In reality, almost every organisation that handles personal data will fall into one of these categories — and sometimes both.

Understanding this distinction is critical because it determines your legal obligations, contractual responsibilities, and level of accountability under Kenyan data protection law.

What Is a Data Controller?

A data controller is any person or organisation that determines:

  • Why personal data is collected, and
  • How that personal data will be used.
In simple terms, the data controller is the ultimate corporate decision-maker regarding the information lifecycle.

Examples of Data Controllers in Kenya

A data controller may include:

  • A law firm collecting client information.
  • An employer managing employee records.
  • A hospital collecting patient data.
  • An online business collecting customer details.
  • A property developer collecting buyer information.
  • A school or university managing student records.

Key Responsibility of a Data Controller

The controller carries primary responsibility for ensuring that personal data is:

  • Lawful Collection Collected transparently and with a valid legal basis.
  • Purpose Limitation Used strictly for clear, declared legal intents.
  • Data Security Kept securely using modern architectural parameters.
  • Retention Boundaries Not retained longer than statutory or operationally necessary.

In essence, the controller is fundamentally accountable for compliance before the ODPC.

What Is a Data Processor?

A data processor is any person or organisation that processes personal data strictly on behalf of a data controller.

The processor does not decide:

  • Why the data is collected.
  • How it will be used.

Instead, the processor follows instructions from the controller.

Examples of Data Processors

Common data processors include:

Cloud & Systems

Cloud service providers, document storage systems, and dedicated data hosting providers.

Operational Services

Payroll service providers, outsourced IT support companies, email marketing platforms, and CRM setups.

Key Responsibility of a Data Processor

A data processor must systematically adhere to the following:

  • Only process data under written instructions from the controller.
  • Implement appropriate technical and organizational security measures.
  • Ensure strict operational confidentiality.
  • Not use or repurpose the data for its own commercial purposes.
  • Assist the data controller in fulfilling its structural compliance obligations.

Can an Organisation Be Both a Controller and a Processor?

Yes.

Many organisations perform both roles depending on the exact processing context.

For example, a law firm is a data controller for its own corporate client files, staff pipelines, and marketing lists. However, it may act as a data processor if it handles and analyzes database arrays on behalf of another organization under a structured legal service agreement.

Similarly, a Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) provider acts as a data processor for the records managed within its systems by its subscribers, but remains a data controller for its own direct employees and B2B sales pipelines.

Why the Distinction Matters

Understanding your specific role is important because it establishes:

1. Your Legal Obligations

Controllers carry broader, strategic compliance responsibility under the law, including ensuring a valid lawful basis for processing, responding directly to Data Subject Access Requests (DSARs), and ensuring public-facing transparency obligations are met. Processors have targeted obligations focused closely on structural security safeguards.

2. Contractual Requirements

Where a third-party processor is engaged, the Data Protection Act legally requires a structured written agreement governing the scope of processing, confidentiality, technical security parameters, sub-processing criteria, and data destruction. These are commonly called Data Processing Agreements (DPAs).

3. Liability Exposure

Controllers generally bear the primary legal liability for institutional compliance failures. However, processors can be held directly liable by regulators if they act outside explicit instructions, fail to implement robust technical security safeguards, or unlawfully manipulate data assets.

Common Mistakes Businesses Make

  • Incorrectly assuming they are always data controllers.
  • Assuming they are never data processors under third-party workflows.
  • Believing service contracts do not require specific data protection clauses.
  • Treating informal, independent freelancers as non-processors.

Practical Scenario Example

A corporate firm hires an external third-party payroll entity:

The hiring corporation determines what employee information is captured and why → Data Controller

The external payroll system handles salaries, deductions, and processing strictly on direction → Data Processor

Relationship to the Data Protection Act, 2019

The Data Protection Act imposes obligations on both controllers and processors, but the level of accountability differs. The Act mandates:

  • Corporate accountability and operations transparency.
  • Lawful processing frameworks for all personal data.
  • Advanced technical and organizational security safeguards.
  • Complete compliance with active data subject rights.
  • Strict structural restrictions on cross-border transfers.

Conclusion

The distinction between data controllers and data processors is fundamental to understanding data protection compliance in Kenya. Most organisations will find that they occupy both roles depending on the nature of their operations. Proper classification is essential for ensuring compliance, reducing regulatory risk, and structuring appropriate contractual safeguards.
